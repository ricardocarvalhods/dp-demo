This is Section 2

We protect the privacy of all.

Just as there is a chance of being the only household on a census block, there are other situations that could put privacy at risk. Adding noise to the data protects against these situations as well.

Let us use dormitories as an example. Imagine two college dormitories on different census blocks.

Dorm A happens to only house 10 male residents, while Dorm B happens to house 1 female resident and 9 male residents.

The woman in Dorm B can be identified in the data, since she would have the only data with “sex = F”. We protect her by adding noise to all group quarters, not just ones with one person at risk, This avoids situations with only members of one group.

Here is where uncertainty comes in again.

Because there is random noise added to the Census reported data, no one can be certain how many women live in either Dorm A or Dorm B. This change has a minimal effect on the accuracy of the data, but protects the privacy of people in these situations.

Protecting people in situations such as this by adding noise to the data is one of the major improvements we have made since the 2010 Census. It is one more way that the new method of adding random noise to the data is providing reliable privacy protection.
