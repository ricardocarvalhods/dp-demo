Protecting Group Quarters

In the last example, we saw how adding noise to data protected the only family on a census block. There are other situations where privacy is at risk, especially because of diverse housing options and the varying populations of each census block.

Any residence not defined as a housing unit by the Census Bureau is called a group quarters, including nursing homes, military barracks, dormitories, and shelters. Group quarter statistics are released separately within the census blocks, making resident data easier to identify and putting them at greater risk.

Let’s use a college dormitory to show how noise protects group quarters.

This dorm is an officially co-ed dormitory, and happens to house 1 female resident and 9 male residents.

Without noise added to these data, someone looking at the data could narrow in on the identity of the only woman in this dorm. For example, the Average Female Age would simply release her personal age. Being the only woman in this group quarters makes her data easier to identify.

We protect her by adding noise to the group quarters.

Knowing that noise has been added to the data, a person does not know if there are one, two, three, or even no women in the dormitory. Noise slightly alters the statistics, and adds this uncertainty about individual data for someone trying to locate specific people’s data.
